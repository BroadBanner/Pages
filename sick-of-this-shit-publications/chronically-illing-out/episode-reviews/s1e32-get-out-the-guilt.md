# Chronically Illing Out | E32 - Get Out The Guilt

## Dr. Greeley Miklashek joins Soso, Beth, and Nick to trace chronic-illness guilt back to a broken human inheritance — and to argue that self-forgiveness is the only road out.

> "Guilt is basically a form of self-punishment for breaking some written or unwritten rule of social behavior."
>
> _\~ Dr. Greeley Miklashek \~_

### Chronic Illness In Review

Dr. Greeley Miklashek is 80, recovering from long COVID and a second round of prostate-cancer radiation, and unwilling to apologize for any of it. He's also a retired neuropsychiatrist who treated 25,000 people, wrote _Stress R Us_, and spent a career arguing that what most of his colleagues called "disease" was, more often than not, a body trying to survive chronic stress. In a wandering, generous hour, he gives Soso, Beth, and Nick a working definition of guilt — self-punishment for breaking the rules of a group — and then complicates it by pointing out that most of us no longer live in the group those rules were written for. The result is one of the more useful episodes the show has done on a topic that usually gets reduced to a slogan: stop feeling guilty for being sick.

The first takeaway is the definition itself. Greeley reframes guilt not as a moral verdict but as a stress-response pattern: a punishment we administer to ourselves when we sense we've violated the social contract, written or otherwise. That framing matters because chronic illness is, almost by design, a violation of unwritten rules — the rule that adults are productive, the rule that partners are reciprocal, the rule that you don't ask for help when other people are busy. Beth puts the abstraction on the floor: this past week's flare left her with no neck movement and forced her to lean on the person she's getting ready to divorce. The guilt didn't come from the divorce; it came from needing him at all. Greeley's answer is to treat that feeling as evidence of misfit, not failure.

The second takeaway is the four F's, and Pete Walker's "fawn" in particular. Greeley names the canonical stress responses — fight, flight, freeze, fawn — and admits, without much romance, that he spent most of his life in the fourth one: making sure everyone else was all right, looking normal, fitting in. The relevance for chronic-illness listeners is direct. Fawn is the response that hides illness from coworkers, performs wellness at family dinners, and over-explains every cancellation. Soso, who names herself a CPTSD sufferer with a psychology background, catches herself fawning on air when she apologizes for keeping Nick on the show through his pain. The diagnosis lands because it's modeled, not lectured.

The third takeaway is the clan. Greeley keeps returning to it — humans evolved in small matriarchal, matrilineal hunter-gatherer groups, he argues, and our nervous systems are still scoped for that scale. In that scale, asking for help is not a transaction; it's the architecture. The modern alternative — independent households, isolated caregivers, soon-to-be-ex-husbands as last-resort nurses — produces guilt because it asks each person to do alone what we were built to do in extended groups. The hosts don't oversell the prescription. Beth offers acceptance ("this is what it is and move forward") rather than utopia, and Nick uses the bottom of the hour to point listeners toward the Save America community's 24-hour Epstein-files reading as a small, real instance of what collective attention can do.

The fourth takeaway is the spiritual one, and it's where the show usually risks losing rigor. Greeley keeps it grounded. Sobriety came at 67. The 12 steps worked when a sponsor took them seriously. Forgiveness of his parents came only after a cousin explained the abandonment trauma both of them carried into his childhood — transgenerational trauma named plainly. The shirt on his back reads, _the earth is my church and nature is my religion_, and the Mound Cemetery swing he walks to most days is his version of a Buddhist temple. The point is not the metaphysics. The point is that self-forgiveness has to attach to something larger than the self, or it doesn't hold.

This is a recurring-guest episode in slow motion — Greeley clearly has more to say, and the hosts are already negotiating the second appearance before the first one ends. Beth dedicates the meditation segment to Robert Frost and roads less traveled; Soso names the rare quiet of finding "someone who knows." Nick, audibly hurting, signs off early to lie down. Next week brings Nieta Greene on disability coalition-building. What this conversation adds to the _Chronically Illing Out_ arc is a vocabulary: guilt as stress response, fawn as default, the clan as the missing infrastructure, and self-forgiveness as the doorway most of the recovery literature gestures at but rarely names. It's worth saying plainly, and Greeley does.
