# Diogenes Club | E12 - Finding Our Groove

## On grief held in public, the architecture of rape culture, and what it looks like when men find their footing together.

> "I can say definitively that the five of us — I'm going to throw Evan in here because I know Evan doesn't feel this way — every person that's been associated with this show or any other platform we participate in: there is not one of us that believes that anything these women have gone through should ever be subjected to that level of abuse. Period. Hard stop."
>
> _\~ Eric Lullove \~_

### Diogenes In Review

The twelfth episode of the Diogenes Club opens with the ambient texture of a live stream still coming together — Evan Fields is caught in technical purgatory, Nick Paro is holding the space, Walter Rhein and Eric Lullove are present and ready. Frederic Poag arrives forty-three minutes in, to a reception that involves Nick reading an epic poem Walter composed in his honor. The episode runs an hour and twenty-two minutes and covers, without a rigid agenda, the full terrain the club has been circling all season: grief, complicity, consent, accountability, the collapse of media as a public trust, and the ongoing project of building something new out of the rubble. If there is a thesis embedded in the title, it is not that the groove has already been found — it is that finding it is the work, and the work is always in progress.

Eric Lullove opens the episode by honoring his grandmother, who died at 104. He does not eulogize her in abstractions. He tells two stories. In the first, she calls him from Florida during the 2000 election to say she thinks the fix is in because the punch card didn't align. In the second, she takes a call from a pollster arguing that marriage should be between a man and a woman, considers the pitch, and responds: "That's okay — I'm a lesbian." She was in her eighties. She was not joking. She was a staunch Democrat who worked for the Palm Beach Democratic Party her whole life, was put on a Smucker's jar at 100, and read books on her Kindle until her mobility slowed. "She was a big part of why I turned out on the right side of the grass." Walter responds by making the case that grief is not a reason to go quiet — it is a reason to speak. His friend lost a son to suicide and told him he wanted to talk about his boy, not around him. "That's how we keep the memory alive. That's the opposite of toxic masculinity." Nick reflects on his own relationship to grandparent loss — one grandfather died when he was four or five, a grandmother developed Alzheimer's — and expresses genuine gratitude that Eric's daughter had eighteen years of relationship with her great-grandmother. "Most kids don't even know their great grandparents." The episode begins, in other words, not with a topic but with a person — and by doing so, it establishes what this room is before it gets to what this room does.

Eric's pivot from grandmother to politics is not accidental — it is the same move the group has been making all season, and it lands hard. We live disconnected from each other *because* of technology, he argues, even as we use technology to try to reconnect. Walter's candidate interview series — which by this episode has grown to include Shelby Campbell (Michigan), Dr. Melissa Bird (Oregon), Emily Berge, and others through the Courage Candidates network — is named as the counter-model: using the platform not to broadcast but to introduce. And from the disconnection thesis, Eric goes directly to the CNN investigation into motherless.com, a coordinated network in which thousands of men shared pharmaceutical techniques for drugging and raping their wives, livestreamed the assaults for a paying audience, and charged viewers $20 a session. He has been holding this for a week. He is not restrained about it. "You fucking piece of shit. That is not how you treat your best friend and life partner." Walter, characteristically, moves from the individual to the system: "The guys who have the power to stop these rapes, to hold these men accountable, are deliberately not doing it. I'm assuming malice. I'm straight up assuming malice." Nick answers the "do you love Israel?" question from the YouTube chat, places himself clearly against the Israeli government's conduct in Palestine, and then refocuses: "Our society has basically accepted rape culture as culture. We cannot sit by idly."

What follows is one of the most careful and difficult passages the Diogenes Club has navigated. Eric tells a clinical story about a new patient — a 42-year-old survivor of marital rape whose ex-husband is in prison, who came to him with a swollen leg and a history of heavy psychotropic medication, whose trauma had made even a standard medical exam a fraught negotiation. He describes going through a "whole separate consent process" — asking if it was okay to proceed, reading her, acknowledging her before touching her. "I'm much more aware of this shit now when I'm seeing female patients." A subscriber in the chat raises concerns about Protected Health Information (PHI). The group does not dismiss the concern. Walter says he'll sit with it. Eric defends his practice — no identifying details, a generic age that could be wrong, a symptom picture without specifics, a story told to illustrate a physician's evolving behavior in the room. Nick reads the room and calls it: Eric addressed the concern and was not gaslit into admitting wrongdoing he did not commit. "If you don't want to listen to the show, you don't have to." The exchange is uncomfortable and instructive simultaneously — this is what it looks like when a group commits to accountability without collapsing under bad-faith pressure, and when they also refuse to simply bulldoze a concern because defending a friend is easier than sitting with it.

Nick's contribution to the consent thread is direct and personal. He has been married eleven years as of Monday. He and his wife practice enthusiastic consent with each other. He posted this on Threads and Substack — the responses were dramatically different. On Substack, readers affirmed it. On Threads, the attacks came from men who wanted to know if his wife wrote it, whether his "husband's boyfriend" needed consent, whether he had his "balls in a ball sack." "Y'all, you guys are the fucking problem." He calls out married men specifically: your spouse's body is not yours. You do not own access to a person. This is not a progressive luxury — it is the minimum floor. "Anything but yes means no." Walter adds his practice: he crosses the street when he sees a woman approaching on the sidewalk, because being safe is not enough if the other person doesn't know it. "I can't walk around being like I know I'm safe. You have to prove it all the time."

The media consolidation section arrives with Evan still missing and a particular kind of grief about that — Nick had wanted to use this episode to help Evan, whose week has been rough and whose experience of getting exploited by larger platforms is exactly what this conversation is about. Eric names it: "Joy Reid is the only established name who has actually helped independent creators. I can't say the same for Midas. I can't say the same for Acosta." Walter's frame is structural: the small papers were bought up, the streaming services predetermine five choices per category, the local candidate running door-to-door can't get a link shared by the website that's still operating. "I've been doing these candidate interviews, and the local newspapers don't share the links. They don't want an informed public." Eric points out that Walter is the only person doing primary-level candidate interviews — that the bigger names wait for the general. Nick announces his first Courage Candidates interview scheduled for the next day: Joseph Perez Caputo, Connecticut 4. Jasmine Thomas, Oklahoma Senate, is mentioned in the chat by a viewer. The Courage Candidates are in a quarter of all House races nationwide. "If we win a quarter of the House of Representatives with progressive candidates who are willing to do what is needed for the people — we change the country."

Frederic Poag arrives mid-episode to the reading of his poem — "I made you into a god. That's what we'll say" — and immediately grounds the energy. He has been on a job interview. He is philosophical about the odds: "There can be only one. I'm hoping I'm that one." His entry into the episode's political thread is immediate: the DOJ inspector general is launching an investigation into Pam Bondi and Todd Blanche's handling of the Epstein files. Maxwell pardon talks are escalating. The group's response is something between rage and dark comedy. Frederic: "If he pardons her, it's going to open it up so she can say whatever the fuck she wants. He's an idiot. I hope he does it." Nick agrees: there's a double-edged sword there. Frederic's cleanest diagnosis of Trump's operating logic came from Michael Cohen: "He's not a grand strategist. He literally doesn't think five past. He's in the present. That's white supremacy." Walter: "White supremacy only exists in the minus two seconds. They will hurt you one day, and the next day they're like, that was the past."

The accountability architecture runs through the entire episode and surfaces most clearly when Nick addresses the Congressional Sexual Assault slush fund directly. He has a public challenge: "We need one male Congress member to put his career on the line and go on the floor and name them. Not a female. A male. With the most privilege in the world. Don't ask for approval. Don't wait for approval. Just do it." Frederic invokes Cory Booker breaking the Strom Thurmond filibuster record: "Why can't Democratic senators just start reading the Epstein files on the floor? You don't even have to do it alone — you can pass it off to someone else." Walter's answer: we have surrendered the media, and even a senator reading those names on the floor would be trashed by every oligarch-owned outlet before the clip finished loading. "That is why you guys being here matters. We have to continue to grow. We are a fledgling. We are a seed."

The episode ends in something close to joy. Eric shares polling data live: Trump is minus 68 on the economy, Republican approval with their own base has collapsed from 93% to 89% in four months, independents have dropped from 35% approval in August to 25% in December — and this is last year's data. Frederic takes partial credit for the independent numbers, having recently re-registered. Nick wraps it: "Fred's being part of the solution." The Diogenes Club's twelfth episode doesn't wrap anything up — the Epstein investigation is ongoing, Evan missed the conversation, Maxwell may get pardoned before the episode posts — but it does something the title promises: it finds the groove. The room knows how to work together now. They know when to hold a hard conversation and when to pass. They know when to call something out and when to call something *for*. That's the groove. That's what twelve episodes builds.
